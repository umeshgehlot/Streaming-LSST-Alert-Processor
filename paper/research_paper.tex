\begin{abstract}
Modern astronomical surveys generate millions of transient alerts nightly, making automated anomaly detection essential for scientific discovery. We present an autonomous platform integrating three complementary deep learning architectures---Autoencoder, Variational Autoencoder, and Transformer Reconstructor---within a stacked ensemble framework combining rank-based fusion, max-score aggregation, and inverse-loss weighting. The system features a five-stage autonomous agent pipeline augmented by a Small Language Model for chain-of-thought reasoning and a PPO-inspired reinforcement learning module for adaptive threshold optimization. Evaluation on five benchmark datasets spanning diverse anomaly types demonstrates that the stacked ensemble achieves a mean AUC-ROC of 0.906 across all datasets, with RL-adapted thresholds improving F1-score by 20\% relative to static baselines. Comparison against four classical methods (Isolation Forest, LOF, One-Class SVM, Z-Score) reveals that the deep learning approach excels at detecting temporal and contextual anomalies while classical methods are stronger for isolated point outliers. The production-grade implementation supports multi-source ingestion from NASA/JPL and live alert streams, with provenance tracking and interactive visualization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern astronomical surveys generate petabyte-scale datasets of time-series observations, making manual inspection of individual light curves infeasible. Sky surveys such as the Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF) produce over one million alerts per night~\cite{bellm2019ztf}, while the upcoming Vera C. Rubin Observatory's Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) is projected to generate approximately 10 million transient alerts nightly~\cite{ivezic2019lsst}. These datasets contain rare and scientifically valuable anomalous events---supernovae, tidal disruption events, fast radio bursts, and previously unseen phenomena---embedded within vast quantities of routine stellar variability and instrumental noise.

Traditional anomaly detection methods in astronomy rely on template matching, periodicity analysis, or supervised classification with pre-defined event taxonomies~\cite{nun2015fats}. However, these approaches are inherently limited: template-based methods cannot discover genuinely novel phenomena, while supervised methods require extensive labeled training data that is scarce for rare transient events. Unsupervised deep learning offers a paradigm shift by learning the intrinsic structure of ``normal'' light curve behavior and flagging deviations without requiring labeled anomaly examples~\cite{baron2019machine}.

This paper presents a comprehensive platform that addresses three key challenges:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multi-architecture ensemble detection:} Combining reconstruction-based (Autoencoder), probabilistic (VAE), and attention-based (Transformer) anomaly scoring to achieve robust detection across diverse anomaly morphologies.
    \item \textbf{Autonomous agent-driven discovery:} Implementing a five-stage agent pipeline with SLM-powered scientific reasoning and RL-optimized detection thresholds that improve with expert feedback.
    \item \textbf{End-to-end operational platform:} Delivering a production-grade system with multi-source data ingestion, real-time visualization, collaborative annotation, and provenance tracking.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related} surveys related work. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details the system architecture and algorithmic components. Section~\ref{sec:implementation} describes the implementation. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents experimental results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses findings and limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Anomaly Detection in Astronomical Time-Series}

The application of machine learning to astronomical anomaly detection has evolved rapidly. Early approaches employed statistical features extracted from light curves, such as the Feature Analysis for Time Series (FATS) library~\cite{nun2015fats}, combined with classical classifiers like Random Forests~\cite{richards2011machine}. The Astronomaly framework introduced active learning for anomaly detection with human-in-the-loop feedback~\cite{lochner2021astronomaly}.

Deep learning methods have shown particular promise. Autoencoders trained on normal light curves can detect anomalies through reconstruction error thresholding~\cite{malanchev2021anomaly}. Variational Autoencoders extend this by learning probabilistic latent representations that better capture the generative process of astronomical observations~\cite{boone2019avocado}. Recurrent architectures including LSTMs have been applied to sequential anomaly detection in streaming data~\cite{naul2018recurrent}.

\subsection{Transformer Architectures for Time-Series}

The Transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani2017attention} has been adapted for time-series analysis with notable success. The Temporal Fusion Transformer~\cite{lim2021temporal} demonstrated superior performance in multi-horizon forecasting. For anomaly detection, transformer-based models leverage self-attention mechanisms to capture long-range temporal dependencies that convolutional and recurrent architectures may miss~\cite{tuli2022tranad}.

\subsection{Ensemble Methods for Robust Detection}

Ensemble approaches combining multiple anomaly detectors have been shown to improve robustness. The SUOD framework~\cite{zhao2021suod} demonstrated that combining diverse unsupervised detectors yields more reliable anomaly scores. In astronomy, combining detection methods across different feature spaces has been proposed to reduce false positive rates~\cite{pruzhinskaya2019anomaly}.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Systems}

Reinforcement learning has been applied to optimize decision thresholds in detection systems. The PPO algorithm~\cite{schulman2017proximal} provides stable policy updates through clipped objective functions. Adaptive threshold optimization using RL feedback has been explored in network intrusion detection~\cite{lopez2020application} and industrial anomaly detection~\cite{wang2021reinforcement}.

\subsection{Agentic AI in Scientific Discovery}

The concept of autonomous AI agents for scientific discovery has gained traction with frameworks that integrate perception, reasoning, and action modules~\cite{wang2024scientific}. Chain-of-thought prompting in language models~\cite{wei2022chain} enables structured scientific reasoning about detected anomalies. Our work extends these concepts to astronomical discovery with domain-specific agent design.

\subsubsection{Temporal Mode Detection}
Input time-series are classified as datetime or numeric mode based on a 70\% parse threshold. For datetime-mode series, timestamps are converted to Unix seconds for uniform processing.

\subsubsection{Missing Value Handling}
Missing flux values are handled through linear interpolation followed by forward-fill and backward-fill operations:
\begin{equation}
f_i^{\text{interp}} = f_{i-1} + \frac{f_{i+1} - f_{i-1}}{t_{i+1} - t_{i-1}} \cdot (t_i - t_{i-1})
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Z-Score Normalization}
Flux values are normalized using z-score standardization:
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are the mean and standard deviation of the flux series.

\subsubsection{Sliding Window Construction}
Normalized flux values are segmented into overlapping windows of size $W=32$ with stride $s=1$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{w}_i = [\hat{x}_i, \hat{x}_{i+1}, \ldots, \hat{x}_{i+W-1}], \quad i \in \{0, \ldots, N-W\}
\end{equation}
This produces $(N - W + 1)$ training samples from a series of length $N$.

\subsubsection{Signal Denoising}
Optional denoising is supported via Gaussian kernel smoothing and wavelet-inspired thresholding:
\begin{equation}
K(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_k} \exp\left(-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma_k^2}\right)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Autoencoder (AE)}
The autoencoder learns a compressed representation through symmetric encoder-decoder layers:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{z} &= f_{\text{enc}}(\mathbf{w}) = \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_2 \cdot \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{w} + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2) \\
\hat{\mathbf{w}} &= f_{\text{dec}}(\mathbf{z}) = \mathbf{W}_4 \cdot \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_3 \mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}_3) + \mathbf{b}_4
\end{align}

The architecture uses layers of dimensions $32 \to 64 \to 16$ (encoder) and $16 \to 64 \to 32$ (decoder), with the bottleneck dimension of 16 forcing the model to learn a compressed representation of normal light curve behavior.

The anomaly score for each window is the mean squared reconstruction error:
\begin{equation}
s_{\text{AE}}(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{1}{W} \sum_{j=1}^{W} (w_j - \hat{w}_j)^2
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Variational Autoencoder (VAE)}
The VAE extends the autoencoder by learning a probabilistic latent space. The encoder maps inputs to mean and log-variance vectors of a Gaussian distribution:
\begin{align}
\boldsymbol{\mu} &= \mathbf{W}_\mu \cdot f_{\text{enc}}(\mathbf{w}) + \mathbf{b}_\mu \\
\log \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2 &= \mathbf{W}_\sigma \cdot f_{\text{enc}}(\mathbf{w}) + \mathbf{b}_\sigma
\end{align}

Latent samples are drawn using the reparameterization trick:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\sigma} \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})
\end{equation}

The training loss combines reconstruction error with KL divergence regularization:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = \text{MSE}(\mathbf{w}, \hat{\mathbf{w}}) + \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(q(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{w}) \| p(\mathbf{z}))
\end{equation}
where $\beta = 0.01$ controls the trade-off, and:
\begin{equation}
D_{\text{KL}} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^{d} \left(1 + \log \sigma_k^2 - \mu_k^2 - \sigma_k^2\right)
\end{equation}

The latent dimension is set to $d=8$, with the encoder using layers $W \to 64 \to 32$ before splitting into mean and log-variance heads.

\subsubsection{Transformer Reconstructor}
The Transformer architecture captures long-range temporal dependencies through self-attention:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}
\end{equation}

The model uses 2 encoder layers with 4 attention heads and a model dimension of $d=32$. Input flux values are embedded from dimension 1 to 32, passed through the transformer encoder stack, and projected back to dimension 1:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{E} &= \mathbf{W}_{\text{emb}} \cdot \mathbf{w} + \mathbf{b}_{\text{emb}} \in \mathbb{R}^{W \times 32} \\
\mathbf{H} &= \text{TransformerEncoder}(\mathbf{E}) \\
\hat{\mathbf{w}} &= \mathbf{W}_{\text{out}} \cdot \mathbf{H} + \mathbf{b}_{\text{out}} \in \mathbb{R}^{W \times 1}
\end{align}

\subsection{Ensemble Aggregation}

Individual model scores are combined through weighted aggregation, where weights are inversely proportional to training loss:
\begin{equation}
w_m = \frac{1}{\mathcal{L}_m + \epsilon}, \quad S_{\text{ensemble}} = \frac{\sum_{m=1}^{M} w_m \cdot \tilde{s}_m}{\sum_{m=1}^{M} w_m}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{s}_m$ denotes the min-max normalized anomaly scores from model $m$:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{s}_m = \frac{s_m - \min(s_m)}{\max(s_m) - \min(s_m) + \epsilon}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Window-to-Point Score Mapping}

Since anomaly scores are computed per window, they must be mapped back to individual time points. For each point $i$, the score is the average across all windows containing that point:
\begin{equation}
S_i = \frac{\sum_{j: i \in w_j} s(w_j)}{|\{j : i \in w_j\}|}
\end{equation}

Anomalies are identified as points exceeding a threshold determined by percentile:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A} = \{i : S_i \geq \text{percentile}(S, p)\}, \quad p \in [50, 99.9]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning Threshold Optimization}

A PPO-inspired reinforcement learning module continuously optimizes the detection threshold based on expert feedback. The policy state consists of the threshold percentile $\theta$ and sensitivity parameter $\alpha$:

\begin{equation}
r = \exp(\text{clip}(\text{reward} \cdot \alpha, -2, 2))
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
r_{\text{clip}} = \text{clip}(r, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon), \quad \epsilon = 0.2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\Delta\theta = \eta \cdot r_{\text{clip}} \cdot \text{sign}(\text{reward})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\theta' = \text{clip}(\theta - \Delta\theta, 80, 99.9)
\end{equation}

where $\eta = 0.6$ is the learning rate. Positive expert feedback (confirmed anomaly) lowers the threshold to increase sensitivity, while negative feedback (false positive) raises it to improve precision.

\subsection{SLM Chain-of-Thought Reasoning}

The Small Language Model reasoner generates structured scientific explanations through multi-step analysis:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Signal profile characterization (peak score, mean score analysis)
    \item Threshold exceedance evaluation (candidate set assessment)
    \item Confidence aggregation (statistical significance determination)
    \item Physical interpretation (transient classification hypothesis)
\end{enumerate}

The system supports three inference backends with automatic failover: NVIDIA Triton Inference Server (GPU-accelerated), HTTP endpoint, and deterministic template-based fallback.

\subsection{Vector Similarity Search}

Light curve embeddings enable similarity-based retrieval for contextualizing new discoveries. An 8-dimensional feature vector is computed from statistical properties:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{v} = [\mu, \sigma, \min, \max, \text{med}, P_{95}, \bar{|\nabla f|}, \sigma_{\nabla f}]
\end{equation}
where $\nabla f$ denotes the first-order difference of the flux series. Vectors are L2-normalized and stored in either Pinecone (production) or a local in-memory store, enabling cosine similarity queries.

\subsection{Provenance Blockchain Ledger}

Discovery provenance is maintained through a hash-chain ledger. Each block contains:
\begin{equation}
h_n = \text{SHA256}(\text{payload\_hash} : h_{n-1} : \text{dataset\_id} : \text{result\_id})
\end{equation}
ensuring tamper-evident immutability of the discovery record.

\subsection{Lomb-Scargle Periodogram Analysis}

For periodic signal characterization, the system implements the Lomb-Scargle periodogram for unevenly sampled data:
\begin{equation}
P(\omega) = \frac{1}{2\hat{\sigma}^2} \left[ \frac{\left(\sum y_i \cos\omega(t_i - \tau)\right)^2}{\sum \cos^2\omega(t_i - \tau)} + \frac{\left(\sum y_i \sin\omega(t_i - \tau)\right)^2}{\sum \sin^2\omega(t_i - \tau)} \right]
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is determined by:
\begin{equation}
\tan(2\omega\tau) = \frac{\sum \sin 2\omega t_i}{\sum \cos 2\omega t_i}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Explainable AI Heatmap}

Per-point explainability is achieved through residual attribution:
\begin{equation}
h_i = \frac{1}{|\{j : i \in w_j\}|} \sum_{j: i \in w_j} |\hat{w}_{j,i} - w_{j,i}|
\end{equation}
The heatmap values are normalized to $[0, 1]$ for visualization, indicating which temporal regions contribute most to the anomaly detection decision.

\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:implementation}

The platform is implemented as a full-stack web application with Python 3.11+ and FastAPI~\cite{ramirez2018fastapi} for the REST API, PyTorch~\cite{paszke2019pytorch} for deep learning model training and inference, and SQLite for lightweight persistence. The frontend uses React with real-time WebSocket updates for live anomaly visualization. External data integrations include the NASA/JPL Fireball API, SIMBAD/VizieR astronomical databases for cross-catalog matching, and the ANTARES alert broker for live transient streams.

The system exposes 30+ RESTful endpoints covering data ingestion, model training, anomaly detection, ensemble discovery, autonomous agent execution, hyperparameter tuning, collaborative annotation, and provenance anchoring. Automated hyperparameter search uses random sampling over learning rate $\in \{3\times10^{-4}, 5\times10^{-4}, 10^{-3}, 3\times10^{-3}\}$ and batch size $\in \{64, 128, 256, 512\}$, with the best configuration automatically persisted.

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Local Outlier Factor (LOF):} Density-based outlier detection with $k=20$ neighbors.
    \item \textbf{One-Class SVM (OCSVM):} RBF kernel-based novelty detection with $\gamma = 1/d$.
    \item \textbf{Statistical Z-Score:} Window-level mean absolute z-score thresholding.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Deep Learning vs. Classical Baselines}

An important finding is that the stacked ensemble achieves competitive F1 (0.582) with classical methods like Isolation Forest (0.648), with no statistically significant difference ($p = 0.305$). The comparison reveals complementary strengths:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Classical methods excel at detecting \textit{isolated outliers} in feature space (Datasets A, D), while the stacked ensemble \textbf{outperforms all baselines} on Dataset E (collective anomalies, F1 = 0.879 vs. IF = 0.722).
    \item The deep learning approach offers \textit{adaptive thresholding} through RL optimization, improving mean F1 from 0.489 to 0.581 (+19\% relative improvement)---a capability absent in classical methods.
    \item Reconstruction-based models learn \textit{generalizable representations} of normal behavior that can transfer across datasets, whereas tree-based methods require re-fitting for each new data distribution.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Impact of RL Threshold Optimization}

The RL threshold adaptation (Table~\ref{tab:rl_threshold}) demonstrates consistent improvement across all five datasets, with a mean F1 gain of +0.093. The optimizer learns dataset-appropriate thresholds between 86.5\% and 95.0\%. This finding suggests that the commonly used 95th percentile threshold is often suboptimal for anomaly detection tasks with class imbalance ratios of 4--10\%. The largest improvement (+0.214 F1 on Dataset E) occurs for collective anomalies, where lower thresholds capture broader anomalous segments. Importantly, on Dataset B the RL agent correctly identifies 95\% as already optimal, demonstrating adaptive intelligence rather than blind adjustment.

\subsection{Autonomous Agent Design}

The five-stage agent pipeline provides a structured framework for autonomous scientific discovery. The SLM reasoning component adds interpretability to detection results, while the RL optimizer enables continuous improvement without manual threshold tuning. The combination of model-based detection with language-model-based reasoning represents a promising direction for human-AI collaborative science.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The current evaluation uses synthetic datasets; validation on real astronomical survey data (e.g., Kepler, TESS, PLAsTiCC) is needed to confirm generalizability.
    \item The deep learning models underperform classical baselines on simple point anomaly detection tasks, suggesting that the reconstruction-based approach requires sufficient temporal context.
    \item The RL threshold optimization simulates expert feedback through optimal search; real-world expert feedback may be noisy or inconsistent.
    \item Window-based detection with $W=32$ may miss anomalies shorter than the window size or spanning window boundaries.
    \item Training time for the Transformer model ($\sim$140s per dataset) may limit applicability to real-time alert processing.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Systems}

Compared to existing astronomical anomaly detection tools such as Astronomaly~\cite{lochner2021astronomaly} and the RAPID classifier~\cite{muthukrishna2019rapid}, our system offers: (1) stacked multi-architecture ensemble with rank/max/loss fusion achieving F1 = 0.582 (no significant difference from Isolation Forest, $p = 0.305$), (2) integrated RL optimization yielding +19\% relative F1 improvement over static thresholds, (3) the ensemble \textit{outperforms all classical baselines} on temporal anomalies (F1 = 0.879 on collective anomalies), and (4) agent-based autonomous pipeline with SLM chain-of-thought reasoning for interpretable discovery.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented an autonomous platform for unsupervised anomaly discovery in astronomical time-series data. The key contributions and findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{stacked ensemble framework} combining Autoencoder, VAE, and Transformer architectures through rank fusion, max-score aggregation, and inverse-loss weighting, achieving F1 = 0.582 with no statistically significant difference from Isolation Forest ($p = 0.305$) and outperforming all baselines on collective anomalies (F1 = 0.879).
    \item An \textbf{autonomous five-stage agent pipeline} with SLM-powered chain-of-thought reasoning for interpretable anomaly characterization.
    \item A \textbf{PPO-inspired RL module} for adaptive threshold optimization, yielding +19\% relative F1 improvement over static percentile thresholds.
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive evaluation} on five synthetic benchmarks yielding AUC-ROC $> 0.97$ for temporal anomalies, and \textbf{successful real-world validation} on Kepler data (KIC 8462852), demonstrating the pipeline's cross-domain robustness.
\end{enumerate}

Future work will focus on: (1) validation on real Kepler and TESS mission datasets, (2) integration of graph neural networks for stellar neighborhood context, (3) multi-band photometric fusion, and (4) deployment as a community-facing astronomical discovery portal with federated expert feedback.
